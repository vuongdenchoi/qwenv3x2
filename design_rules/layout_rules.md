# Layout Design Rules

## Role: Composition, Spatial Organization, and Wayfinding
Composition is the arrangement of visual elements within a field to construct meaning. It controls what the audience encounters first and last, how relationships among elements are perceived, and how the viewer navigates through the design. Every compositional decision — proximity, scale, direction, edge — is a communicative act.

---

### I. Composition and Arrangement

Rule 1 — Composition is never neutral:
Designers arrange elements to form compositions. The meaning of a message depends not only on the choice of particular elements but on their organization within a visual field. Contrasting size, color, shape, location, direction, or movement can draw attention to one element over others and construct a hierarchy of importance among units of information. There is no default or neutral arrangement — every layout makes an argument.

Rule 2 — Establish a deliberate reading entry point:
The Gutenberg Diagram describes how Western (left-to-right) readers typically approach a printed page — entering at the top-left and moving toward the bottom-right, leaving "fallow areas" in the upper-right and lower-left corners. Place the most critical element of the composition along the primary optical path. Avoid placing key content in fallow zones without strong visual cues to redirect attention there.

Rule 3 — Proximity groups, distance separates:
The Gestalt principle of proximity states that elements close to one another appear to form groups, even when they are dissimilar in other ways. If elements overlap or touch, we read them as sharing something in common. If one element encloses another, we read this as a dependent or hierarchical relationship. Use proximity deliberately: increase closeness to suggest relationship, increase distance to signal separation.

Rule 4 — Grouping must be visually reinforced:
Grouping is an orientation strategy that uses proximity or visual similarity to indicate that elements are related and different from other elements. Elements sharing similar color, shape, size, or position are perceived as a unit. Ensure that groupings are intentional — ambiguous groupings create navigational confusion. Labels should group visually with what they label; captions with their images; data points with their category markers.

---

### II. Scale, Proportion, and Size

Rule 5 — Scale communicates relative importance:
Scale is a proportional relationship between the size of one element and the sizes of others. Larger elements are perceived as more important, closer, or more dominant. Make the most critical element the largest and most prominent. Scale also creates spatial illusion — larger elements appear to advance toward the viewer; smaller elements recede.

Rule 6 — Proportion creates internal harmony:
Proportion refers to a consistent relationship among parts of a whole, regardless of total size. Proportional systems (such as the golden ratio or modular grids) establish part-to-whole relationships that govern decisions about element sizing and placement. Avoid arbitrary sizing — every element should be proportioned in meaningful relation to others in the system.

Rule 7 — Size constancy must be tested at final output scale:
Size constancy is our mental ability to interpret the approximate size and scale of objects even when visual evidence suggests otherwise. A design that looks balanced at thumbnail size may be unreadable at actual output dimensions. Always proof layouts at the actual output scale — never approve a design from a screen thumbnail when the final artifact is a large format print or environmental installation.

---

### III. Symmetry, Direction, and Balance

Rule 8 — Symmetry and asymmetry communicate different energies:
Symmetry is a correspondence in size, shape, and arrangement of parts on opposite sides of a plane — it conveys order, formality, and stability. Asymmetry is a lack of this correspondence — it creates visual tension, energy, and dynamism. Neither is superior; the choice must reflect the emotional and communicative intent of the message. Applying symmetry to a dynamic, energetic brand feels false; applying asymmetry to a formal, authoritative context undermines credibility.

Rule 9 — Direction guides the eye along an implied vector:
Direction is the line along which something appears to be moving with reference to a future point. Visual elements — arrows, diagonal lines, gaze directions in photographs, implied motion — create directional vectors. Use direction deliberately to lead the viewer's eye toward focal points, called-to-action elements, or the next unit of information. A composition without directional control leaves the viewer stranded.

Rule 10 — Point of view frames experience:
Point of view is the position from which something or someone is observed. It can frame events, scenes, or information in radically different ways, telling the viewer where they are positioned relative to the subject. A low camera angle implies the subject is powerful; a high angle positions the viewer as dominant. Point of view influences perception and must be chosen to support the intended relationship between viewer and content.

### IV. Depth, Edges, and Space

Rule 11 — Layering creates depth in the flat picture plane:
Layering creates the illusion of depth in a two-dimensional surface through overlapping forms, transparency, size variation, and vertical positioning within the frame. In dynamic media, elements can be layered in time — moving between background and foreground along an imaginary Z-axis. Use layering to add visual richness and to establish foreground-midground-background relationships.

Rule 12 — Edge relationships define boundaries and imply continuation:
An edge defines the boundary between two things: between object and environment, between a composition element and its background, or between two elements. Elements that approach the edge of a frame create tension and imply continuation beyond the boundary. Elements that bleed off the edge suggest a world larger than the frame. Tightly containing all elements within the frame creates a closed, self-contained composition.

Rule 13 — White space is an active structural element:
Negative space (white space) around and between elements is not emptiness — it gives elements room to be seen and read as distinct. It provides perceptual rest, establishes separation, and signals importance (isolated elements receive more attention). Avoid cluttered layouts that compete for attention without establishing hierarchy. Use consistent margins, column gutters, and padding as structural components of the grid system.

---

### V. Orientation and Wayfinding

Rule 14 — Affordances communicate how and where to act:
Affordances are "action possibilities" present in objects or environments — they communicate what is possible and how to use something without requiring instruction. In layout design, affordances guide the viewer: a large button affords clicking; a column of text affords reading; an arrow affords following. Design spatial affordances so the intended interaction is immediately apparent.

Rule 15 — Wayfinding is navigation made visible:
Wayfinding refers to all the ways in which people orient themselves in space and navigate pathways from one place to another — applicable to both physical environments (buildings, cities) and digital environments (websites, apps). Effective wayfinding systems use consistent visual language (color coding, iconography, typography, landmarks) to tell users where they are, where they can go, and how to get back. Always design for the disoriented user, not the expert.

Rule 16 — Mapping uses spatial position to communicate relationships:
Mapping is the activity of representing information spatially. Relationships among parts are expressed by proximity, location, and directional links. In layout: the spatial position of an element communicates its relationship to other elements — top implies priority, left implies start, center implies importance, periphery implies support. Use spatial mapping consistently so that positional conventions carry reliable meaning throughout the system.

---

### VI. Time, Sequence, and Continuity

Rule 17 — Series and sequences require a clear organizing principle:
A series is a collection of elements sharing something in common without implying a particular order. A sequence exhibits a specific order implied by an organizing principle — numbers, time, cause and effect, or narrative progression. In layout: numbered grids imply sequence; icon grids imply series. Use the correct structural format for the type of relationship being communicated.

Rule 18 — Continuity connects compositions across formats:
Continuity is the perception of visual similarities across time and space. In a multi-format design system (website, brochure, social media, signage), consistent use of grid, color, type, and compositional structure signals that all materials come from a single coherent source. Inconsistency across formats breaks continuity and undermines the credibility and memorability of the identity.

Rule 19 — Feedback confirms user action in interactive layouts:
Feedback is information given in response to an action that serves as the basis for continuing, ending, or improving communication. In interactive or navigational layouts (apps, websites, kiosks), every affordance that accepts user input must provide visible feedback confirming that the action was received. Absence of feedback creates uncertainty and erodes trust in the system.
